Chapter 12: Analysis of Quantitative Data

Making Numbers Speak

Step 1: Preparing Your Data

Before analysis can begin, raw information must be systematically organized and converted into a numerical format that a computer can understand.

1. Coding

Assigning numerical codes to non-numerical categories.
Ex: Male = 1, Female = 2; Agree = 1, Neutral = 2, Disagree = 3.

2. Entering Data

Typing the coded data into a file, typically a spreadsheet-like format called a data matrix where rows are cases and columns are variables.

3. Cleaning Data

Checking for and correcting errors in the data file, such as impossible codes (e.g., a "3" for gender when codes are only 1 and 2).

Step 2: Describing One Variable

Univariate analysis is the first look at your data, variable by variable. Its purpose is to describe the basic characteristics of your sample.

Frequency Distributions

A table that shows how many cases fall into each category of a variable. It's the simplest way to see the "shape" of your data. Can also be shown as a bar chart or histogram.

Measures of Central Tendency (The "Typical" Value)

Mode: The most frequent or common score. Best for nominal data.
Median: The middle point or 50th percentile. It's the best measure when there are extreme outliers.
Mean: The arithmetic average. It's the most widely used measure but is sensitive to extreme scores.

Measures of Variation (The "Spread" of Scores)

Range: The simplest measure. The difference between the highest and lowest scores.
Percentiles: The percentage of cases at or below a certain score.
Standard Deviation: The most powerful measure. It tells you the "average distance" of scores from the mean. A small SD means scores are tightly clustered; a large SD means they are spread out.

Step 3: Exploring Relationships

Bivariate analysis looks at the relationship between two variables at the same time. This is where we start to test our hypotheses about how the social world works.

Contingency Tables

A table that shows the relationship between two categorical variables. By converting the raw numbers to percentages, we can see if the categories of one variable are "contingent" on the categories of another.

Scattergrams

A graph that shows the relationship between two interval or ratio variables. Each dot represents one case. The overall pattern of dots reveals the direction and strength of the relationship.

Beyond Two Variables

Often, the social world is more complex than a simple two-variable relationship. Advanced techniques help us understand these complexities.

Multivariate Analysis

This involves looking at three or more variables simultaneously. It helps us see if an initial bivariate relationship holds up after we control for a third variable. This is how we can spot spurious relationships (where a hidden third variable is the true cause).

Inferential Statistics

This branch of statistics answers a crucial question: "Is the relationship I found in my sample real, or is it just due to random chance?" It uses probability to determine statistical significance. A "significant" result (e.g., p < .05) means we are confident the relationship exists in the wider population.
