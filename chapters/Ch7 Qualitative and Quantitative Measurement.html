The Measurement Process

Measurement is how we bridge the gap between our abstract thoughts about the world and the concrete, observable evidence we can collect.

2. Operationalization

Linking a conceptual definition to a specific set of measurement procedures. This is the "how-to" step of measurement.

Two Paths to Measurement

Qualitative and quantitative researchers approach measurement from different directions, but both are systematic and rigorous.

Qualitative Measurement

  • Timing: Occurs *during* data collection. Concepts and definitions are refined as the study unfolds.
  • Direction: Inductive. Moves from empirical observations to abstract concepts.
  • Data Form: Data is in the form of words, images, actions, and symbols.
  • Process: Conceptualization is a process of forming coherent "working ideas" out of the raw data.

Quantitative Measurement

  • Timing: Occurs *before* data collection. All measurement procedures are developed ahead of time.
  • Direction: Deductive. Moves from abstract concepts to empirical measures.
  • Data Form: Data is in the form of numbers.
  • Process: Operationalization involves creating precise indicators (questions, scales) to capture the concept numerically.

The Gold Standards

Every measure, whether qualitative or quantitative, must be judged on two critical criteria: reliability and validity.

‚úÖ Reliability: Is the measure dependable?

+

Reliability means that the information provided by the measure does not vary as a result of characteristics of the measure itself. It's about consistency. A reliable measure gives you the same result over and over again under the same conditions.

  • Stability Reliability: Does the measure deliver the same results across time?
  • Representative Reliability: Does the measure deliver the same results across different social groups?
  • Equivalence Reliability: Do different indicators of the same concept produce consistent results?

üéØ Validity: Is the measure truthful?

+

Validity refers to how well a measure matches the concept it is intended to measure. It's about accuracy. A valid measure actually captures what it claims to capture.

  • Face Validity: Does it make sense "on its face" to the research community?
  • Content Validity: Does it cover the full range of meaning within the concept?
  • Criterion Validity: Does it correlate with an established, external criterion?
  • Construct Validity: Does it behave as expected within a network of theoretical relationships?

Levels of Measurement

In quantitative research, we classify measures into four levels. The level determines what mathematical operations we can perform on the data.

Nominal

Categories are different, but cannot be ranked.
Example: Religious Affiliation (Christian, Muslim, Jewish, Other, None)

Ordinal

Categories can be ranked from low to high, but the distance between them is not precise.
Example: Opinion (Strongly Agree, Agree, Disagree)

Interval

Categories are ranked, and the distance between them is precise and equal, but there's no true zero.
Example: IQ Score, Temperature (¬∞F/¬∞C)

Ratio

Has all the features of Interval, plus a true zero, which allows for ratio comparisons.
Example: Income in Dollars, Age in Years
