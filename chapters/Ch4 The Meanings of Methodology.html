Chapter 4: The Meanings of Methodology

The Three Lenses of Social Science

More Than Just "How-To"

Methodology isn't a list of techniques. It's the entire philosophical system behind your research. It's about the fundamental assumptions you make about the world and how we can know it.

"The confusion in the social sciences... is wrapped up with the long-continuing controversy about the nature of Science."

- C. Wright Mills

The Three Major Paradigms

Social science is not a single, unified field. It contains three major approaches, or paradigms, to conducting research. Think of them as different lenses for viewing the social world.

Positivist Social Science (PSS)

The oldest and most widely used approach, modeled on the natural sciences. It emphasizes discovering objective laws that govern social behavior.

1. Purpose of Research: To discover and document universal causal laws of human behavior.

2. Nature of Social Reality: It's an orderly, pre-existing reality that can be discovered. Reality is "out there," independent of our consciousness.

3. Nature of Human Beings: Humans are rational, self-interested individuals who are shaped by external social forces.

4. Science and Common Sense: Science is fundamentally different from and superior to common sense. Common sense is riddled with bias and logical fallacies.

5. What is a good explanation? A logical, deductive system of interconnected laws (nomothetic). It explains *why* events occur by identifying causes.

6. How to judge an explanation? It must be logical and supported by empirical facts that can be replicated by other researchers.

7. What does good evidence look like? Facts are distinct from belief. Good evidence is observable, precise, and measured quantitatively.

8. Role of Values: Science should be value-free and objective. Values have no place in the scientific process.

Interpretive Social Science (ISS)

Focuses on understanding the socially constructed meanings and interpretations that guide human action. It seeks to grasp the subjective worldview of those being studied.

1. Purpose of Research: To understand and describe meaningful social action (Verstehen).

2. Nature of Social Reality: It's an intersubjective reality, created and maintained through social interactions and shared meanings.

3. Nature of Human Beings: Humans are social beings who create meaning and actively construct their social worlds.

4. Science and Common Sense: Common sense is the very thing researchers study. Science offers a thematic understanding of common sense reality.

5. What is a good explanation? An empathetic understanding of another's worldview (idiographic). It shows how an action makes sense from the actor's perspective.

6. How to judge an explanation? It must be a coherent narrative that resonates with the lived experiences of the people studied.

7. What does good evidence look like? Good evidence is embedded in the context of fluid social interactions. It includes gestures, words, and feelings.

8. Role of Values: Research is value-relative. Values are an integral part of social life and must be understood, but the researcher should remain detached.

Critical Social Science (CSS)

Goes beyond just studying the world to actively trying to change it. It critiques social injustices and aims to empower people to overcome oppression.

1. Purpose of Research: To smash myths, expose hidden truths, and empower people to change society for the better.

2. Nature of Social Reality: A multi-layered reality where the observable surface is shaped by deep, unobservable structures of power and oppression.

3. Nature of Human Beings: Humans are creative, adaptive beings with great potential, but this potential is often trapped by social conditions.

4. Science and Common Sense: Common sense is often "false consciousness"‚Äîa distraction created by power structures to mask the true nature of reality.

5. What is a good explanation? One that demystifies illusions and reveals the underlying structures of power to help people take action.

6. How to judge an explanation? It is judged by its ability to both explain the world and inspire practical action (praxis) that brings about positive change.

7. What does good evidence look like? Facts are theory-laden. Good evidence is informed by a theory that uncovers the hidden structures of reality.

8. Role of Values: All science is inherently value-laden. CSS embraces an activist stance and is explicitly committed to values of empowerment and justice.

Beyond the Big Three

The conversation doesn't end with the three major paradigms. Other critical perspectives challenge and enrich social science.

Feminist Research

Critiques the male-dominated perspective in mainstream science. It emphasizes the lived experiences of women, advocates for gender equality, and focuses on how gender shapes all aspects of social life.

Postmodern Research

Challenges the very idea of a single, objective "truth." It argues that reality is fragmented and composed of multiple, competing narratives. It seeks to deconstruct and expose the power hidden within language and knowledge systems.
