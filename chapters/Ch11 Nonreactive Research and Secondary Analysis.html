Chapter 11: Nonreactive Research

The Science of Unobtrusive Measures

Studying People Without Their Knowledge

Nonreactive research refers to methods of studying social behavior where the people being studied are unaware they are part of a study. This eliminates the problem of reactivity (like the Hawthorne effect), where people alter their behavior simply because they are being watched.

The key advantage: The act of observation does not influence the behavior being observed.

Content Analysis: The Study of Communications

A technique for gathering and analyzing the content of text, pictures, or symbols. The "content" can be anything that people create and communicate.

What Can Be Analyzed?

  • Books, magazines, newspapers
  • Websites, social media posts, emails
  • Speeches, poems, song lyrics
  • Paintings, photographs, advertisements
  • Movies, TV shows, commercials

The Coding Process

Content analysis involves creating a systematic coding system to convert the content into quantitative data. This involves defining your units of analysis and the variables you will measure.

  • Manifest Coding: Counting the visible, surface content (e.g., counting the number of times the word "love" appears).
  • Latent Coding: Looking for the underlying, implicit meaning (e.g., rating how romantic a song's lyrics are on a scale of 1 to 5).

Existing Statistics: Using Data Archives

In this method, the researcher uses large-scale data sets that were collected by governments or other large organizations. The data is already compiled and available for analysis.

Sources of Data

  • U.S. Census Bureau
  • Bureau of Labor Statistics
  • United Nations, World Bank
  • Crime statistics (e.g., Uniform Crime Reports)

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths: Low cost, extensive data coverage, high quality of data collection.

Weaknesses: The researcher is limited to the variables that were already collected, which might not perfectly match their concepts. It's crucial to understand how the original data was collected.

ðŸš¨ Beware the Ecological Fallacy

This is a critical error of logic. It occurs when a researcher has data for a large group (e.g., cities) and makes assumptions about individuals within that group. Just because a city has a high crime rate doesn't mean every individual in that city is a criminal.

Secondary Analysis: Re-analyzing Survey Data

This is a specific type of existing statistics research that focuses on re-analyzing survey data collected by another researcher or organization. It has become a major research method in the social sciences.

Major Datasets

There are huge, high-quality surveys conducted regularly that are available for public use, such as:

  • General Social Survey (GSS)
  • American National Election Study (ANES)
  • Current Population Survey (CPS)

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths: Enormous savings in time, money, and effort. Access to high-quality, nationally representative samples. Allows for replication and new analysis of old questions.

Weaknesses: The data may be complex and require statistical expertise. The available variables might not be a perfect fit for the researcher's theoretical concepts. Data quality can sometimes be an issue.

Ethical Considerations

Even though nonreactive research doesn't involve direct contact with people, it still has important ethical dimensions.

Privacy & Confidentiality

The primary concern is protecting the privacy of individuals, especially when using existing statistics or secondary survey data. Researchers have a moral obligation to ensure that no individual can be identified from the data they publish.

Data Integrity

Researchers must be transparent about the limitations of the data they are using. This includes clearly stating the original purpose of the data collection and acknowledging any potential issues with validity or reliability.
