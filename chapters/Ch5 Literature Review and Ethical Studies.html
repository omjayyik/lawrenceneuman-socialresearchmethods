Where to Find the Literature

Scholarly Journals

+

The primary location for cutting-edge research. These are peer-reviewed, meaning other experts have vetted the articles for quality. Examples: American Journal of Sociology, Social Problems.

Scholarly Books

+

Provide a more in-depth treatment of a topic than journals. They are also peer-reviewed by the publisher.

Dissertations

+

Works of original research completed for a Ph.D. They are a great source for very specific topics and contain extensive bibliographies. Available through databases like ProQuest.

Government Documents & Policy Reports

+

Federal, state, and international government agencies (e.g., U.S. Census Bureau) and private think tanks produce vast amounts of high-quality data and reports.

Part 2: Ethics in Social Research

Research is not a value-neutral activity. It involves real people and has real consequences. Ethical conduct is the moral compass that guides every stage of the research process.

"The researcher is morally and professionally obligated to be ethical, even when research subjects are not... There are no excuses for unethical behavior."

Key Ethical Principles

üõ°Ô∏è Protection from Harm

+

The primary ethical rule. Researchers must protect participants from physical, psychological, legal, and other types of harm. The potential benefits of a study must always outweigh the potential risks.

üìù Informed Consent

+

Participants must volunteer for a study; they cannot be forced. Informed consent means they are given a full and honest description of the research and its potential risks before they agree to participate. For some populations (e.g., children), this requires consent from a legal guardian.

ü§´ Privacy, Anonymity & Confidentiality

+
  • Privacy: Research should not invade a person's private life without their consent.
  • Anonymity: Participants remain nameless. The researcher cannot link any data to a specific individual.
  • Confidentiality: The researcher knows the participant's identity but promises not to reveal it publicly. Data is kept secure.

üé≠ Deception & Debriefing

+

Deception (intentionally misleading participants) should only be used when absolutely necessary and must be followed by a full debriefing, where the researcher explains the true nature of the study and answers any questions.

üë∂ Special Populations

+

Vulnerable groups (e.g., children, inmates, people with disabilities) require extra ethical protections. Researchers must be especially careful to avoid coercion or undue influence when studying these populations.
