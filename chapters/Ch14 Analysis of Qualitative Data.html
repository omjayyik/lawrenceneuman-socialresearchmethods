Chapter 14: Analysis of Qualitative Data

Finding Meaning in Words and Images

A Different Path to Understanding

Qualitative data analysis is less a linear, technical task and more a creative, iterative process of interpretation. Unlike quantitative analysis, it begins during data collection and involves the researcher actively wrestling with the data to uncover deep meaning.

The Coding Funnel

The central task in qualitative analysis is coding—the process of organizing raw data into conceptual categories. This is typically done in three passes, moving from broad themes to a single core concept.

1. Open Coding

The first pass through the data. The researcher reads through everything—field notes, interview transcripts, documents—and identifies preliminary themes, ideas, and concepts. Codes are assigned to this raw data to condense it into manageable categories.

2. Axial Coding

The second pass. The researcher focuses on the initial codes created during open coding. The task is now to look for patterns and relationships *among the codes*, organizing them into a more structured, analytical framework. This is where you begin to build your theory.

3. Selective Coding

The final pass. After extensive analysis, the researcher identifies the single most important theme or concept—the "center of gravity" for the entire study. They then revisit all other data and codes, relating them back to this central, selective code to form a coherent narrative or theory.

Strategies for Analysis

Beyond coding, researchers use several broad strategies to make sense of their data. Click each strategy to learn more.

The Narrative Strategy

+

The researcher constructs a coherent story, weaving together evidence and data to provide a compelling, chronologically ordered account of what happened and why.

Ideal Types Strategy

+

The researcher develops a pure, abstract model (an "ideal type") of a social phenomenon. They then compare their empirical data to this model to see how it conforms or deviates, which helps to reveal key features.

Successive Approximation Strategy

+

An iterative process where the researcher moves back and forth between data and theory, constantly modifying their analysis as they gather more data and develop a deeper understanding. The analysis gets successively "closer" to the truth with each pass.

The Illustrative Method

+

A deductive approach where the researcher starts with a theory and then uses their qualitative data as empirical "illustrations" or examples of that theory in action.

The Analyst's Toolkit

Successful qualitative analysis relies on tools that help the researcher organize their thoughts and see connections in the data.

Analytic Memoing

The crucial process of writing regular, systematic notes to oneself about the data and the coding process. Memos are where the researcher develops their ideas, documents their analytical decisions, and begins to form their theory. It is "thinking on paper."

Conceptual Maps & Diagrams

Creating visual representations of the concepts and their relationships. Diagrams, flowcharts, and maps can help the researcher see complex connections, organize their thoughts, and communicate their findings more effectively.
